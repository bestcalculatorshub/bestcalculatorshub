How to Calculate Cubic Yards

Cubic yards are the standard unit for ordering bulk construction and landscaping materials in North America. When you call a concrete supplier, a gravel yard, or a mulch delivery service, they quote and sell by the cubic yard. Getting the number right before you order prevents two equally frustrating outcomes: running short mid-project or paying to haul away leftover material.

Basic Cubic Yard Formula

Cubic Yards = (Length ft √ó Width ft √ó Depth ft) √∑ 27

All dimensions must be in feet before dividing. Divide by 27 because 1 cubic yard = 3' √ó 3' √ó 3' = 27 cubic feet.

The single most common mistake is entering depth in inches without converting. A 4-inch slab is 0.333 feet deep ‚Äî not 4 feet. Always convert depth to feet first. Our calculator handles this conversion automatically, no matter which unit you select.

Here's the calculation: convert depth to feet (4 √∑ 12 = 0.333 ft), multiply the three dimensions (20 √ó 10 √ó 0.333 = 66.7 cubic feet), divide by 27 (66.7 √∑ 27 = 2.47 cubic yards). Add 10% for waste and you order 2.75 cubic yards ‚Äî round up to 3 yards for a comfortable margin.

When to Use Each Shape

Most projects are rectangular ‚Äî length √ó width √ó depth covers driveways, patios, raised beds, and foundations. But some situations call for other shapes. Use Cylinder for round ponds, tree wells, circular patios, or footing holes. Use Hollow Tube for cylindrical concrete walls or round planters. Use Trapezoid for drainage ditches and berms with sloped sides. Use Triangle Prism for wedge-shaped ramps and embankment fills. Use Cone to estimate a material pile ‚Äî useful when checking how much sand or gravel has been delivered.

For irregular spaces, the simplest approach is to divide the area into simple shapes, calculate each separately, then add the results together. A kidney-shaped pond, for instance, can be approximated as a rectangle plus a semicircle, calculated as a cylinder half.

Waste and Compaction ‚Äî How Much Extra to Order

Never order exactly what you calculated. Waste allowances depend on the material and application:

  • Concrete slabs: Add 5‚Äì10% for spillage, uneven subgrade, and edges
  • Gravel driveways: Add 25‚Äì30% ‚Äî gravel compacts significantly after spreading and traffic
  • Sand base layers: Add 20% for compaction losses
  • Topsoil (new lawn): Add 15‚Äì20% for settling over the first growing season
  • Mulch: Add 15% for settling and decomposition in the first year
  • Fill dirt: Add 25‚Äì35% ‚Äî loose fill compacts dramatically under its own weight

How Many Cubic Yards in a Ton?

Material suppliers sometimes quote by the ton rather than the cubic yard. To convert, divide the total weight by 2,000 (pounds per ton). For concrete: 1 cubic yard = 4,000 lbs √∑ 2,000 = 2 tons. For gravel: 1 cubic yard = 2,700 lbs √∑ 2,000 = 1.35 tons. Conversely, if a supplier quotes gravel at a certain price per ton and you know you need 5 cubic yards, you need 5 √ó 1.35 = 6.75 tons.

How do I convert square feet to cubic yards?
You need a depth measurement to convert from square feet to cubic yards. Multiply your square footage by the depth in feet, then divide by 27. For example, 200 sq ft at 3 inches deep: 200 √ó 0.25 ft √∑ 27 = 1.85 cubic yards.

Does concrete shrink after pouring?
Concrete doesn't compress or shrink significantly after it's poured. Unlike gravel or topsoil, what you pour is essentially what you get.
