Board Foot Calculator Guide

A board foot is the standard unit of lumber volume in North America, used by lumber yards, sawmills, and woodworkers to price and estimate hardwood and specialty lumber. If you're buying oak flooring, planning a deck, or quoting a cabinet job, board feet is the number that matters. Our calculator handles the math instantly ‚Äî including total cost when you enter your lumber price.

Board Foot Formula

Board Feet = (Thickness" √ó Width" √ó Length') √∑ 12

Thickness and width are in inches, length is in feet. One board foot = 144 cubic inches of wood.

The formula is simple, but there's one catch that trips up most people: you need to use the actual dimensions of the board, not the nominal dimensions. A "2√ó4" actually measures 1.5" √ó 3.5" ‚Äî two numbers that produce very different board foot results. The calculator above uses whichever number you type in, so always double-check which dimension system your lumber yard quotes in before entering prices.

How to Calculate Board Feet Step by Step

Working through the formula manually takes four steps. Take a single piece of 1-inch thick, 8-inch wide, 10-foot long cherry board:

Multiply thickness by width: 1" √ó 8" = 8. Multiply that by length in feet: 8 √ó 10 = 80. Divide by 12: 80 √∑ 12 = 6.67 board feet. If the board costs $6 per board foot, the total is 6.67 √ó $6 = $40.02.

When buying multiple boards of the same size, multiply the per-piece result by quantity. For 15 boards of the same cherry: 6.67 √ó 15 = 100 board feet total.

Board Feet vs. Linear Feet ‚Äî Which to Use

The unit you use depends on the type of lumber and what you're buying it for. Board feet measure volume and are used for hardwood lumber, rough-sawn timber, specialty species, and any situation where thickness varies. Linear feet measure only length and are used for dimensional framing lumber (2√ó4, 2√ó6), decking, trim, and molding where the cross-section is fixed.

If your lumber yard quotes hardwood in board feet and you need to compare it to a competitor quoting by the linear foot, multiply the linear foot price by the board feet per linear foot to get an equivalent price. For a 4/4 (1" thick) √ó 6" wide board: 1 linear foot = (1 √ó 6 √ó 1) √∑ 12 = 0.5 board feet. So a price of $2.50/linear foot equals $5.00/board foot.

Waste Allowances ‚Äî How Much Extra to Order

Always order more lumber than your calculation says you need. The right waste percentage depends on what you're building:

  • Simple straight cuts (framing, shelving): Add 5‚Äì10%
  • Furniture with multiple parts: Add 10‚Äì15%
  • Panels with glue-ups: Add 15‚Äì20% (for surfacing after gluing)
  • Figured or highly figured wood: Add 15‚Äì25% (for defect culling)
  • Irregular live-edge boards: Add 20‚Äì30% (uneven edges reduce yield)
  • Flooring: Add 10‚Äì15% for standard rooms, 15‚Äì20% for diagonal layouts

Frequently Asked Questions

How many board feet are in a standard 2√ó4√ó8?
Using nominal dimensions: (2 √ó 4 √ó 8) √∑ 12 = 5.33 board feet. Using actual dimensions (1.5" √ó 3.5"): (1.5 √ó 3.5 √ó 8) √∑ 12 = 3.5 board feet. Construction lumber from a home center is typically priced by linear foot, not board feet, so this distinction mainly matters when pricing hardwood from a specialty dealer.

How do I convert board feet to cubic feet?
Divide board feet by 12. One board foot = 144 cubic inches = 1/12 cubic foot. So 60 board feet = 5 cubic feet. This conversion is useful for calculating shipping weights or storage space.

Can I use this calculator for metric lumber?
Yes ‚Äî the calculator accepts centimeter inputs. For metric lumber, convert your dimensions (cm to inches for thickness and width, cm to feet for length), or use the cm input option and the calculator converts internally. Note that board feet is a North American standard; European lumber is often sold by cubic meters.
