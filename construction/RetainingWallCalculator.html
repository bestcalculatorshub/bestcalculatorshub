Retaining Wall Calculator Guide: Everything You Need to Know

Not sure how many blocks to order for your retaining wall? You're in good company — most first-time builders either over-order by 20% or run short mid-project and have to make an emergency trip to the supplier. This guide walks you through exactly how to calculate retaining wall materials, what type of wall to build, how much gravel to put behind it, and when you need an engineer.

Whether you're building a simple garden border or a full-height property retaining wall, the math is the same. Let's break it down step by step.

What is a Retaining Wall?

A retaining wall is a structure built to hold back soil or rock from sliding down a slope — keeping earth in place where it would otherwise shift into a more natural, less useful shape. Retaining walls are used for slope stabilization, erosion control, creating level terraces for gardens, driveways, and outdoor spaces, and protecting structures from soil movement.

What makes a retaining wall different from a regular garden wall is that it has to resist lateral earth pressure — the constant horizontal force of the soil pushing against its back face. That pressure increases with wall height, which is why taller walls need more engineering, deeper footings, drainage systems, and sometimes geogrid reinforcement anchored back into the hillside.

How to Calculate Retaining Wall Blocks — 3 Steps

Our calculator does all of this automatically, but here's the full math so you understand what's happening behind the scenes.

Step 1 — Count your rows. Divide the total wall height (visible height plus any buried depth) by the height of one block. Always round up — a partial row still requires a full row of blocks. If your visible wall is 4 ft and you're burying the first course 5 inches, your total height is 4 ft 5 in = 4.42 ft. With 8-inch (0.667 ft) blocks: rows = ⌈4.42 ÷ 0.667⌉ = 7 rows.

Step 2 — Count your columns. Divide the wall length by the face length of one block and round up. For a 20-foot wall using 16-inch (1.333 ft) blocks: columns = ⌈20 ÷ 1.333⌉ = 15 columns.

Step 3 — Multiply and add waste. If no cap row: total blocks = rows × columns = 7 × 15 = 105 blocks. If adding a cap row: main blocks = 6 rows × 15 = 90, cap blocks = 15, total = 105. Add 10% for waste: ⌈105 × 1.10⌉ = 116 blocks to order. The calculator handles the rounding, embedded depth, and waste factor automatically.

How Much Backfill Gravel Do You Need?

Backfill gravel is not optional — it's what keeps your wall standing. Without a gravel drainage layer behind the wall, rainwater saturates the soil, increasing hydrostatic pressure dramatically and pushing your wall forward. The standard installation calls for at least 12 inches of crushed drainage gravel placed directly behind the wall for its full height and length.

The volume formula is straightforward: Backfill Volume = Backfill Thickness × Wall Length × Wall Height. For a 4-ft wall, 20 ft long, with 12" of backfill: Volume = 1 ft × 20 ft × 4 ft = 80 ft³ = 2.96 cubic yards. Gravel weighs approximately 84 lbs per cubic foot (1,346 kg/m³), so that's roughly 6,720 lbs (3.36 tons) of gravel to order.

Important: Order 20% Extra Gravel

Loose gravel compacts 15–25% when properly tamped. A pile that looks like plenty at delivery will shrink significantly once you pack it down behind the wall. The calculator's Gravel Compaction Buffer (default 20%) accounts for this — always keep it enabled unless your supplier already accounts for compaction in their quote.

Drainage Requirements for Retaining Walls

Drainage is the single most important factor in retaining wall longevity. A wall with perfect blocks and a bad drainage system will fail within a few years. A wall with cheaper blocks and excellent drainage can last 50 years. Here's what a proper drainage system looks like, from the wall face back:

  • Gravel backfill layer: Minimum 12 inches of crushed stone (¾" clean gravel or similar) placed directly against the back face of the wall for the full wall height and length. This is the primary drainage path.
  • Landscape fabric / geotextile: Placed between the gravel backfill and the native soil behind it. Prevents fine soil particles from migrating into the gravel and clogging the drainage layer over time.
  • Perforated drain pipe: For walls taller than 3 feet, a 4-inch perforated pipe at the base of the gravel layer (wrapped in fabric sock) carries collected water to a daylight outlet at the end of the wall. Without this, water pools at the base and undermines the footing.
  • Weep holes: For mortared or poured concrete walls, weep holes (minimum 1-inch diameter, spaced every 6–10 feet) allow water to escape through the wall face rather than building up pressure behind it.
⚠️ Never point downspouts toward a retaining wall

Concentrated roof runoff directed at the wall soil is one of the most common causes of retaining wall failure. Redirect all downspouts away from the wall and slope any adjacent surfaces so water drains away from the wall base, not toward it.

Do You Need a Permit for a Retaining Wall?

The short answer: if your wall is under 3 feet in most jurisdictions, probably not. If it's over 4 feet, almost certainly yes. Here are the general rules — but always verify with your local building department before construction, as regulations vary significantly by city and county:

  • Under 3 feet: Usually no permit required in most US jurisdictions for residential property. Still check local codes — some municipalities start requiring permits at 2 feet.
  • 3–4 feet: Permit required in many jurisdictions. Some areas allow this height without a permit if the wall is not in a setback zone or near a structure.
  • Over 4 feet: Permit required almost everywhere. A structural engineer review is typically required for the permit application. The engineer must certify that the wall design is adequate for the soil loads.
  • Near a structure or property line: Additional setback rules may apply regardless of height. Most codes require retaining walls to be set back from property lines and from building foundations.

DIY vs. Professional Retaining Wall Installation

The 4-foot rule is a good starting point for deciding between DIY and professional installation. Under 4 feet with a simple, straight design and good soil conditions — most handy homeowners can handle it. Over 4 feet, near a slope, near a structure, or on unstable soil — hire a professional.

DIY is practical when: the wall is under 4 feet, the design is straight (not curved), soil is stable and not saturated clay, there's no structure above or below the wall, and you're comfortable with physical labor and basic tools.

Hire a professional when: the wall exceeds 4 feet in height, the wall is on or near a slope, there's a structure (house, garage, deck) within one wall-height distance above the wall, soil conditions are poor (soft clay, organic fill, high water table), the wall needs geogrid reinforcement, or local code requires an engineered design.

Geogrid Reinforcement — When Is It Required?

Geogrid is a high-strength plastic mesh buried horizontally in the soil behind the wall and connected to the back of the blocks. It anchors the wall into the earth behind it, dramatically increasing the wall's resistance to overturning and sliding. Most manufacturer installation guides require geogrid for any wall over 4 feet tall.

Standard installation practice: geogrid layers are placed every 2–3 courses of blocks starting from the base, extending back into the hillside a minimum distance equal to the total wall height. The exact spacing and extension distance depends on wall height, soil type, and the specific block system being used — always follow your block manufacturer's engineered installation guide or consult a structural engineer.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many blocks do I need for a 4-foot retaining wall?
For a 4-foot wall that is 20 feet long, using standard 8"×16" concrete blocks and a buried first course of 4 inches: Total height = 4 ft 4 in = 4.33 ft. Rows = ⌈4.33 ÷ 0.667⌉ = 7 rows. Columns = ⌈20 ÷ 1.333⌉ = 15 columns. Total = 7 × 15 = 105 blocks. With 10% waste = 116 blocks. Add a cap row (15 caps) and you're ordering ~115–120 blocks total.

How deep should I bury the first course?
The standard recommendation is to bury the first course approximately 10% of the total visible wall height. For a 4-foot wall, that's about 4–5 inches below grade. This buried base course resists forward sliding. Our calculator's "Embedded Depth" input adds these hidden rows to your total block count automatically — don't skip this input or you'll underestimate blocks needed.

What type of gravel is best for retaining wall backfill?
Use ¾-inch clean crushed stone (sometimes called "clean gravel" or "drainage aggregate"). Avoid pea gravel — it migrates too easily. Avoid native soil or sand — they hold water instead of draining it. The gravel should be placed and compacted in lifts (layers) no more than 8 inches thick for best compaction results.

Can I build a retaining wall on a slope?
Yes, but it requires more careful planning. The standard approach is to step the footing down the slope — you maintain a level base while the top of the wall follows the natural grade. The individual sections are calculated the same way, but each section has a different height. Use this calculator for each step section separately and add the totals together.

How do I calculate a curved retaining wall?
For gently curved walls, add 10–15% to your wall length measurement to account for the extra blocks needed on the curve. For tightly curved sections, blocks may need to be cut — add 15–20% waste instead of the standard 10%. Our calculator uses straight-wall math, so account for any curves by increasing the waste percentage in the Green BONUS section.

What is the difference between base gravel and backfill gravel?
Base gravel (leveling pad) goes under the first course of blocks — typically 6 inches deep and twice the block depth wide, providing a level, compactable base. Backfill gravel goes behind the wall after it's built — this is the 12-inch drainage layer our calculator estimates. Both are needed; our calculator estimates the backfill drainage layer. You'll need a separate gravel estimate for the base/footing pad.

How long does a retaining wall last?
A properly built concrete block retaining wall with good drainage lasts 50 to 100 years. Natural stone walls can last 100–200 years or more. Pressure-treated timber walls last 10–20 years depending on climate and drainage. The biggest factor in premature wall failure is always drainage — a wall with inadequate drainage will fail in 5–15 years regardless of block quality.

What is the difference between a segmental retaining wall and a poured concrete wall?
A segmental retaining wall (SRW) is built by stacking interlocking dry-stack blocks — no mortar, no formwork. It's the type this calculator is designed for. A poured concrete wall is cast in forms, typically with steel rebar reinforcement, and requires concrete ready-mix delivery and forming expertise. Poured concrete walls are stronger for a given thickness but cost more and are not DIY-friendly. For residential applications under 6 feet, segmental block walls are the standard choice.
