How Many Sheets of Drywall Do I Need?

The answer to "how many sheets of drywall do I need?" depends on three things: the total area to cover (walls + ceiling), the sheet size you're using, and your waste factor for cuts. How Much Joint Compound Do You Need?

Joint compound (mud) is applied in three coats: the taping coat, second coat, and finish coat. Each coat requires progressively less mud applied over a wider area. The combined total averages about 0.22 gallons per 100 square feet.

  • Taping coat: ~0.053 gallons per 100 sq ft (thin layer to embed tape)
  • Second coat: ~0.073 gallons per 100 sq ft (8‚Äì10" feathered band)
  • Finish coat: ~0.098 gallons per 100 sq ft (12‚Äì16" wide, very thin)
  • Total (all 3 coats): ~0.22 gallons per 100 sq ft
  • A 5-gallon bucket covers: approximately 2,000‚Äì2,200 sq ft total

Buy all-purpose joint compound for the taping and second coats, and lightweight topping compound for the finish coat. Mixing compound in a 5-gallon bucket gives you better consistency than working from the bag.

Professional Installation Tips

Hang ceiling sheets first ‚Äî walls support the edges and make alignment easier. On walls, hang sheets horizontally (the long edge running parallel to the floor). This orientation places the tapered factory edges mid-wall where they're easier to finish, and puts the cut edges at the floor where they'll be hidden by baseboard. Always stagger butt joints ‚Äî never align seams from adjacent sheets.

When screwing, drive the screw just deep enough to create a slight dimple without breaking the paper face. Over-driven screws that break the paper lose holding strength and leave a weak point. Use a drywall dimpler bit to get consistent screw depth every time.

Common Mistakes That Cost Money

‚ùå Avoid These Drywall Errors

Rushing coats: Each mud coat needs 24 hours to dry before the next. Heat or fans help but don't shortcut this.

Too much compound at once: Thick coats crack and shrink. Multiple thin coats beat one thick one every time.

Skipping the primer: Paint won't seal compound properly. PVA drywall primer is required before painting.

Not marking studs: Mark stud positions clearly on the ceiling before sheeting walls ‚Äî you won't be able to see them otherwise.

Drywall Types for Special Applications

  • Standard (white board): Regular gypsum ‚Äî interior walls and ceilings
  • Moisture-resistant (green board): Bathrooms, laundry rooms ‚Äî not rated for shower enclosures
  • Mold-resistant (purple board): Better than green board, suitable for high-humidity areas
  • Type X (fire-rated): Required by code in garages, mechanical rooms, commercial buildings
  • Soundproof drywall: Dense gypsum with viscoelastic layer ‚Äî effective but expensive
  • Cement board: Not gypsum ‚Äî for tile backer in showers and wet areas
