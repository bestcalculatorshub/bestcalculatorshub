Brick Calculator Guide

Whether you're building a garden wall, a fireplace surround, a patio, or an entire home exterior, the most common first question is: how many bricks do I need? The answer depends on three things ‚Äî your wall area, your brick size, and your mortar joint thickness. Our calculator handles all three simultaneously, plus waste, bond pattern adjustment, mortar bag count, and optional cost estimation.

Quick Brick Calculation Formula

Bricks = Wall Area (ft¬≤) √ó Bricks per Square Foot √ó Waste Factor

For standard modular brick with 3/8" joints: approximately 6.86 bricks per square foot. Add 10% waste for a simple wall.

Most errors in brick estimation happen for one of three reasons: using the wrong brick size, ignoring the mortar joint in the calculation, or forgetting to subtract door and window openings. The calculator above accounts for all three automatically ‚Äî plus it adjusts the count for whichever bond pattern you're using, since patterns like herringbone and basket weave require significantly more cuts and produce more waste than a simple running bond.

How to Calculate How Many Bricks You Need

The math behind brick estimation has four steps. First, calculate your wall area: multiply length by height. Second, divide by the coverage area of one brick including its mortar joint ‚Äî for standard modular brick at 3/8" joints, one brick covers 1/6.86 = 0.1458 square feet. Third, round up to the next whole number. Fourth, multiply by your waste factor.

The mortar joint is critical and often overlooked. Without including it, you would calculate the brick coverage area as (7.625" √ó 2.25") = 17.16 sq in. Adding the 3/8" joint on each face gives (8.0" √ó 2.625") = 21.0 sq in per brick. That difference ‚Äî about 22% more coverage per brick with mortar ‚Äî changes your total count significantly. Always include the mortar joint when calculating.

Mortar Calculation ‚Äî How Much Do You Need?

Mortar volume depends on joint thickness and brick size. For standard modular brick with 3/8" joints, each brick requires approximately 0.027 cubic feet of mortar for all three joints (two horizontal bed joints and one vertical head joint). One 80-pound bag of pre-mixed mortar yields roughly 0.45 cubic feet when mixed, which covers approximately 36‚Äì40 standard bricks.

For thicker 1/2" joints, coverage drops to around 30 bricks per bag. For larger bricks like Utility or Norman size, each brick has more mortar surface area, so bags cover fewer bricks. The calculator adjusts mortar estimates automatically based on your selected brick type and joint thickness.

Mortar types by application: Type N (1:1:6 cement/lime/sand) is the standard for above-grade exterior walls. Type S (1:¬Ω:4.5) is used for below-grade and high-load applications. Type M (1:¬º:3) is for foundations, retaining walls, and driveways subject to heavy loads.

Waste Factor ‚Äî How Much Extra to Order

Order more than your exact calculated number. Waste comes from breakage during delivery and unloading, cuts at corners and edges, pattern transitions, and rejected bricks that don't match color or are damaged. The right percentage depends on your project:

  • Simple straight wall, no openings: 5‚Äì7%
  • Wall with 1‚Äì2 windows or doors: 8‚Äì10%
  • Wall with many openings or corners: 10‚Äì15%
  • Curved or radius wall: 15‚Äì20%
  • Basket weave or herringbone pattern: 15‚Äì20%
  • Reclaimed or handmade irregular bricks: 20‚Äì30%

One important rule: always buy bricks from the same production run. Brick color and texture can vary significantly between batches, even from the same manufacturer. If you run short and need to reorder, the new bricks may not match exactly. If anything, order slightly more than your waste factor suggests.

Wall Thickness ‚Äî Single vs. Double Wythe

A single-wythe wall is one brick thick ‚Äî typically 3‚Öù" for standard modular brick. It's used for veneer cladding, garden walls, and decorative features, but is not a structural load-bearing wall on its own. A double-wythe wall is two bricks thick (about 7¬º" to 9"), which can be load-bearing and offers significantly better insulation and soundproofing. Double-wythe walls require roughly twice the bricks per square foot of face area, plus extra bricks for the header courses that tie the wythes together. A cavity wall uses two wythes with a 2"‚Äì4" air gap in between, totaling about 10"‚Äì13" thick. It's the preferred method for modern exterior walls because the cavity prevents moisture from bridging to the interior wythe.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many standard bricks in 100 square feet?
At 6.86 bricks per square foot: 100 √ó 6.86 = 686 bricks before waste. Adding 10% waste brings that to approximately 755 bricks. Always round up and buy a few extra from the same batch.

Can I use this calculator for a patio or floor?
Yes. For a flat-laid patio with bricks on their face (flat side up), the coverage calculation is the same ‚Äî length √ó height (or width √ó depth for a patio) √ó bricks per square foot for your brick's face dimensions. Select basket weave or herringbone in the bond pattern selector to automatically account for the higher waste these patterns produce.

Why does the calculator ask for both wall length and height separately instead of just area?
Because this allows the calculator to calculate door and window deductions accurately. When you check "Subtract Doors/Windows," the calculator subtracts the exact opening area from your total wall area before calculating brick count.
