Rebar Calculator Guide: Everything You Need to Know

Planning a concrete slab and not sure how much rebar to buy? You're not alone. Most people over-order by 20–30% just to play it safe — and that's real money wasted at the lumber yard. This guide walks you through exactly how to calculate rebar, what size and spacing to use, and how to get the right amount the first time.

Whether you're pouring a backyard patio, a new driveway, or a house foundation — the steps are the same. Let's break it down.

What is Rebar?

Rebar — short for reinforcing bar or reinforcing steel — is a steel rod that sits inside concrete before it hardens. Think of it as the skeleton inside a concrete body. Without it, a concrete slab can crack easily under weight, frost heave, or shifting soil.

Those little ridges you see on every bar are called deformations. They grip the concrete from all sides and lock the two materials together. This bond is what turns plain concrete into reinforced concrete — one of the most durable building materials in the world.

Why Does Concrete Need Rebar?

Here's the thing about concrete: it handles pushing forces really well (that's called compression), but it's surprisingly weak when pulled or bent (that's called tension). Rebar handles the tension that concrete simply can't deal with on its own.

Skip the rebar and your slab could crack in just a few winters — especially in areas with freezing temperatures, heavy rain, or soft ground. A properly reinforced concrete slab, on the other hand, can easily last 50 years or more.

How to Calculate Rebar — Step by Step

Calculating rebar for a concrete slab follows the same three steps every time. Our free rebar calculator above does all of this automatically, but it helps to understand what's happening behind the scenes.

Step 1 — Find your grid dimensions. Rebar doesn't go all the way to the slab edge. You need to leave a gap — called edge clearance or concrete cover — on all four sides. The ACI 318 standard requires a minimum of 1.5 inches indoors and 2 inches for slabs exposed to weather. Subtract twice the edge clearance from both your slab length and width to get your actual grid size.

Step 2 — Count bars in both directions. Rebar is placed in a grid with bars running in two directions. Divide the grid dimension by your on-center spacing, then add 1. That "+1" accounts for the starting bar at the first edge. Multiply each bar count by the distance those bars span to get your linear footage in each direction.

Step 3 — Get pieces and weight. Add your overlap and waste percentage (10% is the standard for most residential work), divide the total footage by your stick length, and always round up. Never round down — you'll run short mid-pour. Multiply total footage by your bar's weight per foot to get the total weight for freight or cost estimates.

What Size Rebar Do You Need?

Choosing the wrong bar size is one of the most common rebar mistakes. Too small and your slab won't have enough strength. Too big and you're spending more than you need to.

Here's the simple rule most contractors follow: use #3 rebar for patios and sidewalks. Use #4 rebar for driveways, garage slabs, and standard home projects — it's the most popular size in residential construction. Use #5 rebar for house foundations, heavy footings, and retaining walls. When in doubt, go one size up. The cost difference is small but the strength gain is significant.

Rebar vs. Wire Mesh — When to Use Which

Both rebar and welded wire mesh reinforce concrete, but they work differently. Rebar is stronger, more flexible in how you lay it out, and better for anything that carries heavy loads. Wire mesh is cheaper, faster to install, and good enough for simple, light slabs.

Use rebar when: you're pouring a driveway, garage floor, foundation, footing, or retaining wall — basically anything a vehicle drives on or anything structural. Use wire mesh when: you have a small garden path, thin decorative pour, or light-duty patio under 100 square feet with no vehicle access. If you're unsure, go with rebar. The extra cost is minimal but the extra strength is worth it every time.

Lap Splice / Overlap Length Explained

A standard rebar stick from your supplier is usually 20 feet long. If your slab is wider than 20 feet, you'll need to overlap two bars at the joint. This overlap is called a lap splice, and the length it needs to be is set by your bar size.

Per ACI 318, the minimum lap length equals 40 times the bar diameter. For #4 bar (½" diameter): 40 × 0.5" = 20 inches minimum. Most contractors use 24–30 inches to stay comfortable. The Overlap & Waste field in the calculator sets this automatically — the default 10% covers lap splices and cutting waste for most residential projects.

Quick Lap Splice Reference

#3 bar: 15–22" minimum  |  #4 bar: 20–30" minimum  |  #5 bar: 25–38" minimum  |  #6 bar: 30–45" minimum

How Much Extra Rebar Should You Order?

The calculator gives you the theoretical minimum. But real job sites aren't theoretical. Always add extra to cover lap splices at joints, off-cuts that can't be reused, and any measuring surprises once your forms are set.

The 10% rule is the industry standard for residential projects. For complex shapes or large slabs with many required splices, bump that up to 12–15%. Always round up to the nearest full stick — you can't send cut pieces back. Running short mid-pour is a much bigger problem than having two extra bars left over.

Rebar Cost — What to Expect in 2026

Rebar pricing tracks the steel market, so it shifts over time. As a general guide for 2026: #3 rebar runs roughly $0.40–$0.65 per foot. #4 rebar is around $0.55–$0.85 per foot. #5 rebar is approximately $0.85–$1.25 per foot. Prices are higher at big-box stores and lower per foot when buying in bundles from a steel yard or wholesale supplier.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between Grade 40 and Grade 60 rebar?
Grade 40 rebar has a minimum yield strength of 40,000 psi. Grade 60 has a minimum yield strength of 60,000 psi — which is why it's the standard for most structural work in the US today. Grade 60 is stronger for the same bar size. Grade 40 is softer and easier to bend on site, so it's still used where field bending is needed.

Does rebar rust inside concrete?
Standard carbon steel rebar can rust if moisture and oxygen reach it through cracks or thin concrete cover. This causes the concrete to crack and flake — called spalling. Keeping adequate cover thickness (1.5"–3" depending on exposure) and using epoxy-coated or stainless steel rebar in wet, salty, or outdoor environments significantly reduces this risk.

Can I use rebar for a retaining wall?
Yes — rebar is essential for concrete retaining walls. You'll need both vertical bars (typically #4 or #5 at 12" on center) and horizontal bars tied across them. For any retaining wall taller than 3 feet, get an engineer to review the design. The soil pressure on taller walls increases dramatically and proper sizing matters a lot.

How many pieces of rebar come in a bundle?
It depends on bar size and supplier. At a steel yard, typical bundles are: #3 — 60 to 80 pieces, #4 — 40 to 60 pieces, #5 — 25 to 40 pieces. Big-box stores usually sell individual sticks or small packs of 5–10 pieces. Buying by the bundle from a steel yard saves a significant amount per foot versus retail pricing.

Do I need rebar for a 4-inch concrete slab?
It depends on use. A 4-inch decorative patio with foot traffic only — wire mesh may be fine. A 4-inch driveway or garage floor that cars will park on — use rebar. ACI 318 allows wire mesh as minimum reinforcement for non-structural slabs on grade, but rebar adds meaningful crack resistance, especially in freeze-thaw climates or on unstable soil.

How long does rebar last inside concrete?
In dry indoor conditions with proper concrete cover, rebar can last 50 to 100+ years without any significant corrosion. In harsh environments — near the coast, in parking garages with de-icing salts, or in constantly wet conditions — service life without special coatings can drop to 20–40 years. That's exactly why epoxy-coated and stainless steel rebar exist for those applications.
