Brick Calculator Formula — How It Works

Here is the thing — the math is simple once you see it clearly.

The Formula

Number of Bricks = Wall Area ÷ Brick Face Area (mortar joint included)

Adjusted Total = Brick Count × (1 + Waste %)

What each part means: Wall Area is your wall length × height in square feet. Brick Face Area is the space one brick fills on the wall face, mortar joint included. Waste % covers cuts, breakage, and fitting around openings.

The mortar joint matters more than most people expect. Without it, a US Modular brick covers about 17.2 sq in. Add a standard 3/8" joint and that becomes 21.0 sq in — roughly 22% more coverage per brick. Always include the mortar joint when calculating, or your count will be too high.

Worked Example — 10 ft × 8 ft Garden Wall (US Modular Bricks):
Step 1 — Wall area: 10 × 8 = 80 sq ft.
Step 2 — Brick count: 80 × 6.75 = 540 bricks.
Step 3 — Add 5% waste: 540 × 1.05 = 567 bricks.
Step 4 — Mortar: 567 × 0.02 = 11.3 cu ft ≈ 19 bags of 60-lb mortar.

Mortar Estimation — How Much Mortar Do You Need?

The good news is — mortar math is simple once you have your brick count.

Mortar Formula

Mortar Volume = Number of Bricks × 0.02 cubic feet

For 1,000 bricks: about 20 cubic feet of mortar — roughly 25 bags of 60-lb pre-mixed mortar, or 18 bags of 80-lb.

Bag SizeCoverage per BagBricks Covered (approx.)
60-lb bag~0.45 cu ft~22 bricks
80-lb bag~0.60 cu ft~30 bricks
94-lb (1 sack)~0.75 cu ft~37 bricks

Not all mortar is the same. Choosing the wrong type causes long-term problems. Use Type S for most exterior and structural walls — Quikrete Type S and SAKRETE Type S are widely available. Use Type N for above-grade interior or non-structural walls. Use Type M below grade or where the wall contacts soil, like a retaining wall. For DIY mixing, a standard 1:4 ratio of Portland cement to building sand works for most garden walls.

Once you have your adjusted brick count, round up to the nearest full pallet. A standard US pallet holds 500–525 modular bricks. A partial pallet order always costs more per brick — and bricks delivered later may not match your original batch color.

Wall Thickness — Single vs Double Wythe

A single-wythe wall is one brick thick — about 3-5/8" for standard modular brick. It works for veneer cladding, garden walls, and decorative features. A double-wythe wall is two bricks thick (about 7-1/4" to 9"), which can be load-bearing and offers better insulation. Double-wythe walls require roughly twice the bricks plus extra for header courses that tie the wythes together. A cavity wall uses two wythes with a 2"–4" air gap, totaling about 10"–13" thick. This is the standard for modern exterior walls because the cavity blocks moisture from reaching the interior wythe. Wall ties are required for cavity walls — about 2.5 ties per square meter of wall face.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many bricks do I need for a wall?
Multiply your wall length by height to get square footage. Then multiply by 6.75 for US Modular bricks with a 3/8" mortar joint. Add 5% waste. For a 10 ft × 8 ft wall, that is about 567 bricks. The brick calculator at the top does this instantly.

How many bricks are in a square foot?
For US Modular bricks with a 3/8" mortar joint, you need 6.75 bricks per square foot. Larger bricks like the Utility size need only about 3.0 per square foot. Brick size and mortar joint thickness both change this number significantly.

How do I calculate mortar for bricklaying?
Multiply your brick count by 0.02 cubic feet to get mortar volume. For 1,000 bricks, that is about 25 bags of 60-lb pre-mixed mortar. Use Type S mortar for most exterior projects. Brands like Quikrete and SAKRETE are widely available.

What is a waste factor in brick calculation?
A waste factor accounts for bricks broken during cutting, handling, and fitting. Use 5% for straight walls, 10% for walls with openings or corners, and 15–20% for herringbone or curved patterns. Always round your final total up to the nearest full pallet.

How many bricks do I need for a 10×10 patio?
A 10×10 ft patio covers 100 sq ft. Using standard 4" × 8" pavers in a running bond, you need about 450 bricks. Add 10% waste for edge cuts, bringing the total to about 495. Use engineering brick or a paver brick rated for freeze-thaw cycles for any outdoor paving.

How many bricks come on a pallet?
A standard US pallet holds 500–525 modular bricks. Larger bricks like Queen or King size typically pack 250–350 per pallet. Always confirm pallet quantity with your supplier before ordering — it varies by manufacturer and brick size.

How much does it cost to build a brick wall?
A brick wall costs $8–$18 per square foot for materials and labor combined in the USA. Standard red bricks run $0.50–$0.90 each. Labor runs $5–$12 per square foot depending on complexity. A 10×10 ft single-wythe wall costs roughly $800–$1,800 installed, based on 2024–2025 pricing.

How many bricks do I need for 100 square feet?
For 100 sq ft of single-wythe wall using US Modular bricks, you need about 675 bricks. Add 5% waste and order 710–720 bricks. This gives you a safety buffer for cuts and breakage without over-ordering a full extra pallet.

How many courses of bricks make one foot?
Using US Modular bricks — 2-1/4" height plus a 3/8" mortar joint equals 2-2/3" per course — there are exactly 4.5 courses per foot. For UK Standard bricks (65mm + 10mm joint = 75mm per course), there are about 4 courses per foot.

Can I use a brick calculator for a retaining wall?
Yes — enter the retaining wall's length and height as you would for any wall. Retaining walls over 3–4 ft often require engineering approval and may need double-wythe or reinforced construction, which doubles your brick count. Use Type M mortar for any wall in contact with soil.
